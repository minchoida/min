Lenme lets individual investors fund small personal loans (USD $50–$5,000) directly through a mobile app. When I joined, the product had 40K+ App Store ratings — solid traction, but the investor flow had grown organically and never been holistically designed. My job was to rebuild it from research up: understand where investors struggled, make confident design decisions, and ship a measurably better experience.

02 · Problem Definition

Where investors were dropping off — and why

Before redesigning anything, I mapped the existing funnel with analytics + 8 user interviews. The data showed a consistent drop at two points: evaluating a borrower and submitting an offer.

Root causes identified

1

Opaque risk signals

Lenme Score shown as a raw number with no context. Investors couldn't calibrate "is 74 safe?" without a reference frame.

2

Decision paralysis at offer

Submit offer screen required manual APR entry — a term most users didn't fully understand, causing abandonment.

3

No feedback loop post-offer

Once an offer was submitted, investors had no visibility into status, creating anxiety and low repeat engagement.

Why this change
5/8 interviewees said they didn't know what APR to type, and 3 abandoned at this exact screen. I introduced a market-average default (pulled from Lenme's own dataset) with a slider for adjustment. Showing estimated return in plain dollars — not percentage — made the outcome tangible. This change alone drove most of the +13% offer conversion lift.

Why this change
Users could not translate "Lenme Score: 74" into a decision. I added a plain-language label ("Low risk / Good / High risk") next to every score, and surfaced the strongest trust signal — past repayment history — as a visible badge at the top of the profile. Average time to tap "Submit Offer" from the profile screen dropped from 47s to 28s in post-launch sessions.

06 · Final Solution

The redesigned investor flow

Five screens. Each designed around a single decision the investor needs to make.

01

Loan marketplace

Color-coded avatars for instant credit signal. Filter by risk tier, amount, duration, repayment history. Scannable cards with just the essentials.

02

Borrower trust snapshot

Score + plain-language label. Repayment badge surfaced up top. Financial health trend (income vs. expenses) for stability read.

03

Submit offer (redesigned)

Single modal. Smart defaults. Sliders. Estimated return in plain dollars. One-tap confirm for repeat users.

04

Pending offer management

Real-time status: "Borrower has seen your offer." Timestamp. Inline edit / retract. Removes post-submit anxiety.

05

Portfolio dashboard

Loan states (pending / active / completed / default). Month-by-month repayment schedule. ROI per loan visible at a glance.

08 · Reflection

What I learned

Defaults are design decisions

Choosing what value appears in a blank field is one of the highest-leverage design choices. A smart default communicates domain knowledge, reduces decision fatigue, and tells users "this is normal." We underestimate how often a blank field is a full stop.

Plain language > precision

Showing "Lenme Score: 74" meant nothing to first-time investors. Adding "(Low risk)" next to it took 0 engineering effort and significantly changed behavior. Fintech products often optimize for precision at the expense of comprehension.

The post-action state is part of the design

I underestimated the anxiety of "what happens after I tap submit." Building a status layer post-offer wasn't in the original scope — users surfaced it in testing, and it ended up being a meaningful retention driver.

What I'd do differently

I'd push for a usability test of the borrower profile before shipping — the trust snapshot design was validated qualitatively, but I didn't have quantitative task-completion data. Better instrumentation earlier would have made the A/B story cleaner.
